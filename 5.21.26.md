# What to read next?

Many of the titles that currently interest me are related to the concept of managing constraints (or limited resources). I think part of the problem is that I feel very much like a ping-pong ball that's been let loose in a gravity-free room at high speed. I can't maintain any direction for long, and I'm constantly feeling buffeted about by forces both within and outside my control.

The idea of picking just one book to read, therefore, is comically ridiculous. 

## Reading @ Work

I do acknowledge that I will need to read "Leadership Is Language," both for my own edification because people are talking about this book at work, and I never got the chance to participat in the course, and also because this book is on our book club list for the Leadership Academy.

I then come to the next question, when should I set aside time to read this book? I like the idea of taking the first hour of my day and reading. I also don't think I need to devote an entire hour each day to this practice. If I read ~5 pages per day, I need ~56 days to finish the book. That gets me through 8/12/2026. If I want to finish the book by 7/15/2026 (a doable goal), I would need to read ~8 pages per day. I think I like that better.

That still leaves me with ~30 minutes to use for professional development. I think I would like to use that to work my way through the GTD Redux course I designed for myself to get my work and life back on the rails (more or less).

## Reading Outside Work

I have a couple reasons I read outside of work. One is to engage with nonfiction material related to a topic I'm curious about. This is mostly personal/professional development, but not always. I also like to read for pleasure.

Ideally, I would spend some time before work reading nonfiction books. I would then allow my down time and evenings to be about fiction books, for fun. 

## The Issue

The primary issue is the constraint I identified while listening to Cal's interview with David Epstein. In his interview, they discuss the Theory of Constraints, and how to apply this concept to your personal/professional selves. I immediately identified my sleep routine as a huge constraint. Because I don't get to sleep at a more reasonable hour, I end up sleeping until I have to get up for work, leaving no time to pursue my personal ambitions.

I've taken to writing after going to my bedroom at night, and while this practice has lead me to writing more in recent weeks than I have in recent years, the truth is, I don't really start my wind-down until after 10 pm. In fact, even if I am tired at 9 pm when I go to my bedroom, my writing wakens me enough that I have to wind back down again. This is an obvious issue. 

So, I think I will design a tiny experiment.

### Observation

I am unable to wake up early enough to take advantage of any early-morning mojo for personal ambitions. I get good, quality sleep most of the time, I'm just going to sleep way to late to get up earlier than is absolutely necessary for my work.

### Question

How can I get to sleep earlier while preserving my quality sleep?

### Hypothesis

Swapping when I write with my bedtime might help me move the needle on my sleep habits.

### Tiny Experiment

For the next month, I will turn off the lights to sleep by 10 pm. As a side-note, these are the activities I won't engage in prior to bed:
- reading the news
- writing about the news

If I feel compelled to write, I will write down what I'm grateful for that day. If I feel the need to read something, I will read either a physical book or on my kindle. I won't read on my phone. I am allowed to play games, as this tends to make me drowsy, but it's lights out at 10 pm. 

I won't worry about waking up any earlier than I have in the past. My current alarm is set for 6:30 am. I'll leave this alone for now, as it's likely to take me longer to fall asleep at first. Once I decide if this is working, my next experiment will be with pulling my wake time earlier.

## Conclusion

Notice how my tiny experiment didn't require I add another book to my repitoire just yet. This will likely mean I return Anne-Laure Le Cuff's book to the library, and I will recheck it out when I'm ready for it. The bottom line is, I need to first address my energy issues before I try to add anymore reading to my days.
